5G technology will play a major role in the advancement of autonomous vehicles. Some of the keyways in which 5G will help make autonomous cars a reality include: (Website)

1) Enhanced connectivity: 5G networks will provide much faster and more reliable connectivity than previous generations of mobile networks. This will allow autonomous cars to have more robust communication with each other and with the cloud, enabling real-time data exchange, coordination and decision making.

2) Low Latency: 5G networks will have very low latency, meaning the time it takes for data to be transmitted and received will be much shorter. This is crucial for autonomous vehicles, which need to receive and respond to information about their environment in real-time to ensure safe and efficient operation.

3) Increased bandwidth: 5G networks will also have significantly greater bandwidth than previous generations, which will allow autonomous vehicles to receive and transmit large amounts of data. This will be necessary for real-time processing of sensor data from cameras, LiDAR and other sources, as well as for transmitting high-definition maps and other important information.

4) Improved reliability: 5G networks will provide increased reliability and availability, which will be crucial for autonomous vehicles, which will need to operate even in areas with weak or spotty network coverage.

Overall, 5G technology will play a critical role in making autonomous vehicles a reality by providing the connectivity, speed, bandwidth, and reliability that these vehicles need to operate safely and effectively. (Website)

"The Human Genome Project was an ambitious international research effort with the goal of mapping and sequencing the entire human genome"[3]. The project was officially launched in 1990. It was a collaborative effort among scientists from various countries. (Website)

Human Genome Project (HGP) includes :-

• Mapping the human genome: Creating a rough map of the genome involved locating the approximate position of all genes on each chromosome, which helped guide further sequencing.

• Sequencing the genome: Sequencing the human DNA involved determining the precise sequence of base pairs that make up the human genome. This was achieved through a mix of automated and manual sequencing techniques.

• Assembling the genome: The raw DNA sequence data was then put together to form a complete, continuous genome by merging overlapping data from various parts of the genome.

• Labeling the genome: The final step was to recognize and label all the genes in the human genome, which required assigning a function to each gene based on its sequence and expression information.

The Human Genome Project has given many benefits in terms of research and medicines. It has provided a comprehensive reference for the human genome, which has greatly improved our understanding of human biology and genetics. The HGP has paved the way for new genetic tests and therapies for a wide range of diseases, including cancer, heart disease, and inherited genetic disorders. Moreover, it has contributed to the development of personalized medicine, which shapes medical treatments to the individual needs of patients based on their genetic information and has also provided new insights into human evolution and the diversity of human populations.
